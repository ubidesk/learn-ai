# Learning-Science Principles

These principles govern how every lesson in Learn AI is designed. They
are drawn from cognitive science, expert-novice research, and hard-won
lessons from teaching complete beginners.

## 1. Prior knowledge is not assumed — it is built

Beginners fail not because concepts are hard but because prerequisites
were skipped. Each lesson explicitly lists what it depends on, and every
dependency has already been taught inside Learn AI.

## 2. Intuition before formalism

A learner should be able to describe *what a thing does and why it
matters* before they see its formal definition, notation, or code. Names
and symbols are labels for intuitions the learner already has.

## 3. Concrete before abstract

Every abstraction is introduced through at least one worked, concrete
example. Generalization comes after the learner has seen the pattern in
the specific.

## 4. Active retrieval and spaced practice

Lessons include low-stakes retrieval checks, not just readings. Concepts
resurface in later lessons in slightly new contexts so memory
consolidates.

## 5. Worked examples, then faded scaffolds

New skills are demonstrated in full, then practiced with progressively
less support (the "fading" of scaffolds), then finally exercised
independently on realistic problems.

## 6. Cognitive load is a design constraint

We split content, avoid split-attention effects, and never introduce two
new hard ideas simultaneously. Extraneous cleverness (fancy words, cute
metaphors that mislead) is cut.

## 7. Metacognition is taught explicitly

Learners are shown how to notice confusion, how to debug their own
understanding, and how to know when they actually know something.

## 8. Feedback is fast, specific, and kind

Every practice item explains not just whether the learner is right, but
*why*, and what to try if they are wrong.

## 9. Transfer is engineered, not hoped for

Skills are exercised in multiple contexts and combined with earlier
skills so learners can apply them to new problems, not just recall the
original example.

## 10. Honesty about difficulty and hype

We tell learners when something is genuinely hard, when a topic is
optional, and when an industry claim is marketing. Trust is the
foundation of long-term learning.
